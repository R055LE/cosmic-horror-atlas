# William Hope Hodgson

**Active:** 1907–1918
**Tradition:** Edwardian weird; sea horror; far-future eschatology

## Why he's in the atlas

Hodgson did cosmic horror before the term existed and on a scale most later writers do not attempt. *The Night Land* projects civilization-scale dread across millions of years and across geography that operates as theology. *The House on the Borderland* compresses similar material into a single isolated structure and a journey through cosmic time. Both are uneven as prose; both are foundational as structural moves.

Worth studying:
- Scale — Hodgson's dread operates at geological and cosmological time, not at single-night scale
- Place as antagonist (the Night Land itself, the House)
- Sea horror as a distinct mode — *The Boats of the Glen Carrig*, the Sargasso stories. The ocean as a place where ordinary physics and ordinary causality stop applying.
- Public domain — quote freely

The known weakness is the prose register, especially in *The Night Land*, which adopts a faux-archaic style that most modern readers cannot get through. Read for the structural moves, study an editor's abridgment if needed.
