# Algernon Blackwood

**Active:** 1906–1951
**Tradition:** British supernatural fiction; nature-mysticism strand of weird

## Why he's in the atlas

Blackwood is the cleanest demonstration in the canon that *place* can be the antagonist. *The Willows* and *The Wendigo* do not need monsters in the conventional sense — the landscape itself is hostile in a way that is not metaphorical. The Danube island in *The Willows* is doing the work that an entity would do in a Lovecraft story, and arguably doing it better, because the absence of a named threat keeps the cosmos genuinely unintelligible rather than just elaborately catalogued.

Worth studying:
- Geography-as-character without anthropomorphizing
- The *length* of his unease — Blackwood's stories build slowly, by accumulation, not by reveal. The dread is structural rather than scenic.
- Restraint about the supernatural's nature. Things happen. They are not explained. They are not even named.
- Public domain — quote freely when analytically useful.

His supernatural-investigator stories (the John Silence series) are weaker and less load-bearing for this atlas. Focus on the standalone weird tales.
