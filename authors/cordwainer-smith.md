# Cordwainer Smith

**Active:** 1928–1966 (most major work 1948–1966)
**Tradition:** American science fiction; the Instrumentality of Mankind sequence. Far-future mythopoeic SF.

## Why he's in the atlas

Smith's *Instrumentality of Mankind* stories operate at the same depth-of-history register as Tolkien but in a science-fictional frame, and several of the stories edge directly into cosmic horror without being labeled as such. "A Planet Named Shayol" is one of the bleakest pieces of mid-century SF. "Scanners Live in Vain" treats human transformation as a wound that survives the cure. The whole sequence is told as if remembered from a much later civilization, with names and references the reader is expected to feel rather than understand.

Worth studying:
- Far-future framing as a permission to omit — Smith leaves out the connective tissue and the gaps do work
- Stories told as fragments of a vanished mythology, with the mythology itself unreconstructed
- Dread arising from *human* projects (the Instrumentality, the underpeople) rather than from external entities
- The sense that the reader is reading a translation, possibly garbled, of something originally in a register no one alive can recover
- In-copyright but most of the major stories are widely anthologized — paraphrase and analyze, quote sparingly
