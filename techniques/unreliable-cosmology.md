# Unreliable Cosmology as Horror Mechanic

**Type:** technique
**Sources:** H.P. Lovecraft (the mythos in its original incoherent form); the Derleth reorganization as the failure case
**Tags:** mythos, epistemic-horror, lovecraft, cosmic-indifference
**Related:** [inherit-structure-not-surface](../principles/inherit-structure-not-surface.md), [invisible-scaffolding](../techniques/invisible-scaffolding.md), [lovecraft](../authors/lovecraft.md)

## Summary

If a mythology's premise is that its rules are incomprehensible to humans, then internal inconsistency increases the horror rather than damaging it. Different witnesses see different shapes; different texts give incompatible names; different stories give contradictory genealogies. The reader who tries to systematize fails — and that failure is the point.

## Analysis

Lovecraft's mythos was never a system. It was a deliberate refusal of system. Across his stories, Yog-Sothoth, Azathoth, Nyarlathotep, and the rest are described in mutually incompatible terms. They have no fixed pantheon, no consistent powers, no settled relationships to one another. A given entity is, in one story, a blind idiot god at the center of chaos, and in another, something accessible through a particular chant in a particular dialect. These are not authorial errors. They are the texture of a cosmos that humans cannot model.

The horror lands precisely because of the inconsistency. A reader who tries to draw a chart and fails has *enacted* the in-fiction failure of the cultists, scholars, and protagonists who tried the same thing and broke against it. The frustration is the experience the work is delivering.

August Derleth, who took over Lovecraft's literary estate, did not understand this. He reorganized the mythos into a tidy structure with elemental affinities, opposed pantheons of Great Old Ones and Elder Gods, and a more or less Christian moral arc grafted underneath. Each entity got a clear allegiance and a clear element. The result is legible. It is also no longer cosmic horror. Once the cosmos is explicable, the horror is just a haunted house with a bigger floor plan.

This pattern recurs in modern derivative work, especially in game design. Game systems require systematization — that is the deliverable. Stat blocks, faction relationships, power tiers. RPG sourcebooks for Cthulhu material almost always Derleth-ize by structural necessity. Players need rules; rules require coherence; coherence destroys the cosmology.

The technique, applied positively:

- Write contradictions deliberately, not by accident. A note that "in some accounts X is the consort of Y; in others, the rival; the Selenarum manuscript suggests they are the same entity wearing different masks" is doing the work that "X is rank 3 of pantheon B" undoes.
- Different witnesses are reliable to themselves and contradict each other. Both accounts are real. The contradiction is real.
- Resist the urge to author a true version. There is no true version. If the writer privately knows which witness is correct, the writing will signal that, and the horror will leak.
- When systematization is forced by medium (game mechanics, an in-fiction document the reader sees in full), present the system as one *attempt* at description, explicitly labeled as partial and possibly wrong, rather than as the truth of the cosmos.

The technique generalizes beyond Lovecraft. Any mythology whose premise is human incomprehension benefits from this discipline. Any mythology whose premise is divine order requires the opposite.

## Application

Any project that pairs a systematized output (a game, an RPG sourcebook, a published bestiary, a wiki) with cosmology-based horror is structurally the Derleth problem. The system must be coherent to function. The cosmology must remain incoherent to horrify.

The resolution is to frame the systematic output as one in-fiction *attempt* at cataloguing — by some scholar, sect, or institution within the world — rather than as authoritative. Game pieces are entries in a flawed bestiary, not the bestiary itself. The deeper material can then contradict the published system in places, and those contradictions are features, not bugs.
